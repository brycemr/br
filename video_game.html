Offshore Wind Farm Optimization

A gradient descent optimization of an offshore wind farm implemented using Julia.

Objective

As a personal project, I wanted to build a first-person puzzle video game prototype in which the rooms of the building rearrange themselves as the player moves throughout the game. I also wanted the player to be able to collect and interact with items and puzzles. With more time I want to develop a full-length game, which will need to be able to incorporate data storage that saves the state of the game when the player exits and reloads this data when the player starts the game up again.

Technical Details

I programmed this optimizer in Julia, using the FLOWFarm and SNOW libraries from Brigham Young University’s FLOW Lab. These libraries have built-in functions for performing sparse non-linear optimizations and calculating important properties related to wind farms, such as wake interference between turbines and annual energy production. These libraries do not currently have methods for dynamically calculating the foundation costs for the turbines in an offshore wind farm, so I created functions to generate different depth patterns and calculate the cost of a turbine’s monopile foundation given a specified depth, wind speed, turbine dimensions, and yield stress. To estimate the cost of the monopile, the program calculates the diameter and thickness of the monopile based on the yield stress of steel and the largest bending moment caused by the wind load on the turbine, factoring in the length of the beam required for a given depth. The beam's diameter, thickness, and length can then be used to find the beam's mass, which can then be converted into a cost and used in the optimization of the LCOE. The optimization is performed using a gradient-based descent and automatic differentiation to find the most favorable action for the optimizer to take in each iteration.

Results

The functions that I created to calculate the Levelized Cost of Energy for an offshore wind farm, taking into account the monopile foundation costs under varying conditions, were validated as my model returned values within 3% of those found in a paper published by the National Renewable Energy Laboratory that I based my monopile modeling approach on. The 3% variance can be accounted for by slight differences in their assumptions for the cost of the tower and wind data compared to the values assumed in my model. My optimizer was also successful in the main objective of decreasing the LCOE. For the initial test state with a constant depth of 23.5m, the optimizer improved the LCOE of the wind farm by 8.45%. When running tests with several depth patterns varying from 10 to 60m, the optimizer improved the LCOE by 8.99% on average. The Julia code I developed can be found on my Github.
